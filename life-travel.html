Living with the Land: The Ivatan Way

The Ivatans

The Ivatan people inhabit the northernmost islands of Luzon, including the Batanes and Babuyan groups, which lie in the typhoon-prone region of the Philippines. Itbayat is the largest island, home to around 3,551 people, while Batan Island, the administrative center, has a population of about 6,000. Only the larger islands are suitable for settlement, and even these are mostly rugged and hilly.

The isolation of the islands has allowed the Ivatan to develop a distinct culture influenced by Cordillera societies and the peoples of Formosa, marked by strong self-sufficiency and resilience.

Ivatan Culture

Vakul

Vakul is a traditional headgear designed to protect the wearer from the harsh sun and heavy rains typical of Batanes

Talugong

Talugong is a traditional hat or salakot made from woven palm leaves, designed to shield the wearer from the sun and rain

Kanayi

Kanayi is a traditional vest typically made from woven fibers and designed for durability and comfort in the region's climate

The Ivatan Life

The Ivatan lifestyle, architecture, farming methods, and fishing practices are shaped by constant strong winds. Their culture emphasizes traditional, megalithic practices and self-sufficiency, eliminating the need for marketplaces within the community.

Architecture

Houses are constructed with thick stone walls and traditionally layered thatched roofs to withstand typhoons

Farming

Agricultural fields are often bordered by trees serving as windbreakers

Fishing

Unlike typical Philippine dugout boats, Ivatan fisherfolk use sturdier, rowed boats suited for rough seas

Festivals of Batanes

Vakul-Kanayi Festival

Held every late April in Sabtang Island, Batanes. This festival recognizes the Vakul, a traditional head covering, and the Kanayi, a durable raincoat. More than just weather gear, these garments embody the resilience and cultural identity of the Ivatan people.

Payuhuan Festival

A festival derived from the Ivatan word “payuhuan,” meaning cooperation or bayanihan. It is a week-long celebration held every mid-June in Basco, Batanes and marks the founding anniversary of the province. This features the Ivatan Olympics, their traditional sports, and the Kanan Nu Kumavahayan or Unity Lunch, a communal feast symbolizing togetherness.

Palu-Palo Festival

It is celebrated every July 24 in Basco, Batanes. The festival's name comes from “palo-palo,” meaning sticks or arnis, which the early Ivatan people once used in defending their land against Spanish and Dutch colonizers. During the celebration, locals wear traditional attire made from smoothed tree bark and native materials, symbolizing their courage, unity, and deep connection to Ivatan heritage.
